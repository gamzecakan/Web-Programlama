Twist Dance

The Twist a dance inspired by rock and roll music, emerged as a global phenomenon from 1959 to the early 1960s. Initially considered provocative, it garnered immense popularity worldwide while also sparking controversies from critics. The dance served as inspiration for various other dances such as the Jerk, the Pony, the Watusi, the Mashed Potato, the Monkey, and the Funky Chicken, but none achieved the same level of popularity.

Origin

In the early 1960s, Dick Clark played a significant role in popularizing the dance craze known as "The Twist" through his television show American Bandstand. Initially, Clark recognized the potential of the song when he heard Hank Ballard's original version. However, due to Ballard's perceived raunchiness, Clark convinced Philadelphia record label Cameo/Parkway to record a new version with the more wholesome Chubby Checker. Checker's rendition of "The Twist" was released in the summer of 1960 and became a chart-topping hit in the United States that year and again in 1962. During the height of the twist craze in 1961, the Peppermint Lounge in New York City became a hotspot for twist dancing, with patrons grooving to the house band, Joey Dee and the Starliters. Their song, "The Peppermint Twist," reached number one on the U.S. singles chart for three weeks in January 1962. Bo Diddley also contributed to the twist phenomenon in 1962 with his album Bo Diddley's A Twister, featuring tracks like "The Twister," "Bo's Twist," and "Mama Don't Allow No Twistin'," which addressed the objections some parents had to the dance's pelvic motions. In Latin America, the twist gained popularity in the early 1960s, partly fueled by Bill Haley & His Comets. Their recordings of "The Spanish Twist" and "Florida Twist" found success, especially in Mexico. Chubby Checker, credited by Haley in interviews, coincidentally appeared in two musicals titled Twist Around the Clock and Don't Knock the Twist, named after films Haley made in the 1950s.

Etymology

The dance known as the "twist" roots dating back to the nineteenth century, with its origins tied to a pelvic dance motion brought to America from the Congo during slavery. Early blackface minstrelsy featured a hit song called "Vine Twist" by Joel Walker Sweeney, and the early twentieth century saw dance crazes like the "Mess Around," described in Perry Bradford's 1912 hit "Messin' Around." During this time, the twist primarily involved grinding the hips, and references to the dance, sometimes metaphorical for sex, appeared in songs like Bo Carter's "Twist It Babe" in 1931 and Jelly Roll Morton's "Winin' Boy Blues" in the late 1930s. In the 1953 song "Let the Boogie Woogie Roll," Clyde McPhatter and the Drifters also mentioned the twist in relation to dancing. The familiar and simple dance we now associate with the twist emerged in the late 1950s among teenagers. Chubby Checker played a crucial role in popularizing it, preparing to debut the song"The Twist" to a national audience on August 6, 1960, on The Dick Clark Show. This show, unlike Clark's daytime American Bandstand, was a Saturday night stage program with a sitting audience. Checker's performance and subsequent chart-topping success helped propel the twist to become a cultural phenomenon in the early 1960s.

Steps

The twist dance is characterized by a specific posture and set of movements. Participants typically stand with their feet about shoulder-width apart, with the torso squared to the knees and hips or turned at an angle. The arms are held out from the body, bent at the elbow. The dance involves rotating the hips, torso, and legs as a single unit on the balls of the feet, with the arms staying relatively stationary. The feet grind back and forth on the floor, and the speed, intensity, and vertical height of the dance can be varied. The dance posture is generally low, with little vertical motion, although occasionally one leg may be lifted off the floor for styling. The twist incorporates various moves, including the mashed potato, swimming, drowning, twisting, arm swing, and single leg twist. Chubby Checker, who popularized the dance, described the moves in the following way:

  1. STANCE:Adopt a prizefighter position with one leg extended forward and arms extended forward from the elbow.

  2. MOVEMENT: Swivel the hips from side to side, as if rubbing oneself with a towel. Bend the knees slightly. As the hips move left, the arms move to the right, and vice versa.

  3. FOOT MOVEMENT: Twist the feet as if putting out a cigarette. The entire body moves forward and back and from side to side.

According to Time magazine, the twist is characterized by dancers barely touching each other or moving their feet. The upper body sways forward and backward, and the hips and shoulders twirl erotically, while the arms perform piston-like motions. This description captures the unique and expressive nature of the twist dance, which played a significant role in the early 1960s dance craze.
