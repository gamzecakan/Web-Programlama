Waltz Dance

The waltz is a classical ballroom dance that originated in the Southern German-speaking areas of Austria and Bavaria during the 17th century. It is a smooth, elegant, and graceful dance characterized by a distinctive 3/4 time signature, where dancers move in a closed position, rotating in a series of natural and reverse turns.

History

The roots of the waltz can be traced back to the 16th-century Europe, with references to a sliding or gliding dance, including the volte, that eventually evolved into the waltz. Printmaker Hans Sebald Beham and French philosopher Michel de Montaigne made early mentions of such dances in the 16th century, describing close holds and face-to-face contact. Around 1750, a couples dance called Walzer became popular among the lower classes in Bavaria, Tyrol, and Styria. While the upper classes continued dancing minuets, the lower classes embraced the waltz, and it spread from the countryside to the city suburbs. In the late 18th century, the waltz shocked some aristocrats. In Sophie von La Roche's 1771 novel, a character criticized the "shameless, indecent whirling-dance of the Germans." Vienna, particularly around the 1780s, played a crucial role in popularizing the waltz. It became fashionable in Vienna and quickly spread to other countries, reaching England in 1791 and being introduced to Sussex by German Legion soldiers during the Napoleonic Wars. The waltz gained prominence in Britain during the Regency period, with Dorothea Lieven's endorsement making it respectable. Thomas Raikes noted the sensation it caused in English society in 1813. Lord Byron penned a sardonic tribute to the dance that same year, and influential dance master Thomas Wilson published a description of the correct method of waltzing in 1816. Despite initial resistance, the waltz found acceptance even in exclusive clubs like Almack's in London, although it was considered "riotous and indecent" until as late as 1825. The waltz's closed position became a model for the creation of numerous ballroom dances, and over time, various folk and ballroom waltz variations emerged.

Variants

In the 19th and early 20th century, numerous different waltz forms existed, including versions performed in 3 4, 3 8 or 6 8 (sauteuse), and 5 4 time (5 4 waltz, half and half). In the 1910s, a form called the Hesitation Waltz was introduced by Vernon and Irene Castle.It incorporated "hesitations" and was danced to fast music. A hesitation is basically a halt on the standing foot during the full waltz bar, with the moving foot suspended in the air or slowly dragged. Similar figures (Hesitation Change, Drag Hesitation, and Cross Hesitation) are incorporated in the International Standard Waltz Syllabus. The Country Western Waltz is mostly progressive, moving counter clock wise around the dance floor. Both the posture and frame are relaxed, with posture bordering on a slouch. The exaggerated hand and arm gestures of some ballroom styles are not part of this style. Couples may frequently dance in the promenade position, depending on local preferences. Within Country Western waltz, there is the Spanish Waltz and the more modern (for the late 1930s- early 1950s) Pursuit Waltz. At one time it was considered ill treatment for a man to make the woman walk backwards in some locations. In California, the waltz was banned by Mission priests until 1834 because of the "closed" dance position.Thereafter a Spanish Waltz was danced. This Spanish Waltz was a combination of dancing around the room in closed position, and a "formation" dance of two couples facing each other and performing a sequence of steps."Valse a Trois Temps" was the "earliest" waltz step, and the Rye Waltz was preferred as a couple dance.

Waltz Steps

  1. Box Step(Natural Turn):
    • Start with the closed dance position, where the leader's left hand holds the follower's right hand, and the leader's right hand is on the follower's back.
    • The basic step involves a box pattern: forward-left, side-right, close-left, backward-right, side-left, close-right.
    • Repeat the box step, creating a continuous flow.
  2. Whisk:
    • Begin in the closed position.
    • The leader takes a step to the side (left for the leader), while the follower takes a step forward with the right foot.
    • The leader then closes the right foot to the left foot, and the follower closes the left foot to the right foot.
    • This creates a quick and elegant side step.
  3. Change Steps
    • In the Waltz, dancers often incorporate a variety of changes of steps to add complexity and style.
    • This might involve variations in the footwork, spins, or sway movements.
