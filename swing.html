Swing Dance

Swing dance is a diverse collection of social dances that emerged alongside the swing style of jazz music from the 1920s to the 1940s. Various swing dance styles were developed during this period, including Charleston, Balboa, Lindy Hop, and Collegiate Shag. The Lindy Hop, originating in Harlem in the early 1930s, is the most widely recognized among them today. While many swing dances originated in African-American communities, some, like Balboa, developed outside of these communities but were influenced by the swing era.The term "swing dance" wasn't commonly used until the latter half of the 20th century.

Original forms dating from the 1920s and early 1930s

  1. Charleston
    • Danced partnered or solo, often to faster music.
    • Includes positions like side-by-side, hand-to-hand, and tandem.
    • Features "jockey position" where partners face forward without breaking apart.
    • In side-by-side Charleston, partners only connect at their hips and arms.
    • Leader steps back onto their left foot, while the follower steps back onto their right.
  2. Balboa
    • An 8-count dance done in closed position.
    • "Pure Balboa" evolved in southern California dance halls with limited space and strict codes.
    • Characterized by an upright posture with partners standing chest to chest.
    • Step variations focus on rhythm, style, and changes in direction.
    • Often danced to fast jazz, with both pure Balboa and Bal-Swing considered part of the dance.
  3. Lindy Hop
    • Evolved in the early 1930s and has African American origins.
    • Characterized by an 8-count basic, the "swingout," transitioning from open to closed positions.
    • Emphasizes improvisation and the ability to include other steps in 8-count and 6-count rhythms.
    • Named "Lindy Hop" after an interaction with "Shorty" George Snowden and a newspaper writer.
    • Popularized at the Savoy Ballroom in Harlem, leading to the formation of professional troupes.
  4. Collegiate Shag
    • Primarily danced to uptempo swing and pre-swing jazz music.
    • Believed to have originated in the Carolinas in the 1920s and spread across the U.S. in the 1930s.
    • Still danced today by swing dance enthusiasts worldwide.
    • Considered the first and most popular swing dance of the original swing era.
In summary, these dances reflect the diversity and creativity of social dances during the specified time period, each with its unique characteristics, music preferences, and historical background.

Forms dating from the late 1930s and early 1940s

  1. St. Louis Shag (or "Shag"):
    • A dance that evolved from the Charleston.
    • Originated in St. Louis, Missouri.
    • Features a stationary 8-count basic, often composed of triple-step, kick, triple-step, kick.
    • A fast closed-position dance usually performed to stomp, jump, and boogie-woogie music.
  2. Big Apple:
    • Serves as both a partner dance and a "called" circle dance.
    • Originated in the Afro-American community in the early 20th century.
    • Speculated to have been created in the early 1930s by African-American youth at the Big Apple Club in Columbia, South Carolina.
  3. Little Apple:
    • A partnered version of the Big Apple.
    • Believed to have originated in the Southern United States, particularly in the Carolinas.
In summary, these dance forms emerged and developed during the late 1930s and early 1940s. St. Louis Shag, Big Apple, and Little Apple represent various rhythms and styles prominent in the music and dance scenes of that era.

Swing Dancing Today

Swing dancing, which reached its peak popularity in the 1930s and 1940s, remains vibrant and active in contemporary times. The dance moves have evolved in sync with changing music styles. The influence of swing dancing evident in various other dance forms such as disco, country line dancing, and hip hop. Today,swing dancing and contests continue to thrive globally. The American Bop Association (ABA), a non-profit organization consisting of 34 member swing dance clubs, plays a crucial role in preserving and promoting a diverse range of dance and music styles. These styles include bop, various forms of swing, jitterbug, and shag. The ABA actively contributes to the ongoing legacy and appreciation of swing dancing within the dance community.
